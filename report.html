Theoretical model

A theoretical model is usually developed to explain a situation or a phenomenon. In our case, it's useful to understand and be able to describe an artwork.
Firstly, we identified a set of sub-areas related to our starting point, the Artwork, which are:

  • the Historical Context;
  • the related Events;
  • the Content;
  • the Symbols;
  • the Interpretations;
  • the Debates around the interpretations.

Artwork

We tried to answer to the questions around the metadata of the artwork (title, author, medium, etc.). In addition, we focused on the process of creation, through questions related to the place, the purpose and the historical context of the creation.

  • What is the title?
  • Who is the author?
  • Which art movement does the author belong to?
  • What is the type of artwork?
  • What is the medium?
  • What are the dimensions?
  • Where is it located?
  • When was it created?
  • Where was it created?
  • For what purpose was it created?
  • Which historical period is it related to?

Historical Context

We decided to go deeper into the historical context of the artwork, since it often has a grade role to play as to what concerns the artwork itself.

  • What happened during this period?
  • Where did it happen?
  • When did it happen?
  • Why did it happen?
  • Who was involved?
  • What is the event related to the entity?

Event

From the questions of the Historical Context, it became clear to us that a particular event could have a direct relation with the artwork. Therefore, we described it properly.

  • What happened during this event?
  • Where did it happen?
  • When did it happen?
  • Why did it happen?
  • What is the activity?
  • Who was involved?

Content

We asked ourselves how to describe the content of an artwork, starting from its objective description and then moving to the subject to which it is related. Then we focused on the elements present in the content, analysing them and their relationships.

  • What is the objective description of the artwork?
  • What topic is it related to?
  • What is the subject of the artwork?
  • How does the artwork fit the main subject?
  • What is the element involved?
  • With what does it interact?
  • What does the element represent?
  • What do they represent as a group?

Symbol

Objects of the Content could have a symbolic meaning. We tried to build a schema to describe the objective and metaphorical explanation of the symbol and link it to the related interpretations.

  • What is it represented by?
  • What does it represent metaphorically?
  • What is the interpretation?
  • Where does it get the meaning from?

Interpretation

We analysed everything that concerns a particular interpretation: its content, its author and source, its reception and the relationship with other interpretations.

  • How is it considered?
  • What is the hypothesis?
  • Who is the author?
  • What is the basis of the hypothesis?
  • Where is this hypothesis contained?
  • What is the similar interpretation?
  • What is it involved in?
  • Who sustain the interpretation?

Debate

In this last area, we layed emphasis on the disputes which can arise from the contrast between different interpretations of the same symbol.

  • What is the object of the debate?
  • What is the description of the debate?
  • What are the interpretations involved?
  • What is the prevaling interpretation?

Conceptual model

Once we had defined the questions related to our central interest, we answered to them by creating triples (Subject - Predicate - Object), using existing ontologies: CIDOC-CRM, RDF, DC, EDM, FOAF, ArCo. In particular, CIDOC-CRM helped us in creating the structure, since it's an ontology devoted to the interchange of varied cultural heritage information.

On the figures below, the highlighted cells stand for classes and properties that we have answered to using our ontology.

Our ontology: SIDRO

SIDRO goes to integrate the description one could wish to provide of an artwork through the existing ontologies of the domain. It gives the possibility to describe the content and to shift towards the metaphoric level by talking of what the content represents, i.e. the symbolism. The different interpretations that arise from the content and the symbolic level can be described through the ontology, as well as the debates that emerge between them.

We created the ontology through Protegé and then we imported it on Widoco to produce the documentation draft.

Our sources

Having chosen Guernica as our case study, we went further to select a number of texts which present many contradictory interpretations around the content and the symbolism of the painting. These texts are the following:

  • Carla Gottlieb (Winter, 1964-1965) The meaning of the Bull and Horse in Guernica, Art Journal, 24:2, 106-112;
  • Eugene B. Cantelupe (1971) Picasso's Guernica, Art Journal, 31:1, 18-21;
  • Rudolf Arnheim (1981) Russell: Picasso's Guernica: The Labyrinth of Narrative and Vision, Studies in Visual Communication, 7:2, 84-88;
  • Ora Attia (2011) Separation and individuation in Picasso’s Guernica, The Internation Journal of Psychoanalysis, 92, 1561–1581;
  • Herschel B. Chipp (1973) Guernica: Love, War, and the Bullfight, Art Journal, 33:2, 100-115;
  • Jordi Xifra (2018) Publicizing atrocity and legitimizing outrage: Picasso’s Guernica, Public Relations Review, 44:1, 28-36.

Automatic Knowledge Extraction

By using knowledge extraction, our purpose was to automatically retrieve the symbols present in the text of an interpretation. Moreover, we wanted to perform sentiment analysis on those portions where the symbol is present to understand the position of the author with respect to the symbol itself. With these information, we assumed to be able to look for the presence of a certain symbol in other works and compare the authors' statements.

To reach these goals, we used a series of Python functions.

First of all, we needed to open the file either in .txt, .pdf (through PyPDF2) or by inputting it directly as a string:

            #txt
            text_txt = open("sample.txt", "r")
            text = text_txt.read()

            #pdf
            with open('sample.pdf','rb') as pdf_file, open('sample.txt', 'w') as text_file:
              read_pdf = PyPDF2.PdfFileReader(pdf_file)
              number_of_pages = read_pdf.getNumPages()
              for page_number in range(number_of_pages):
                page = read_pdf.getPage(page_number)
                page_content = page.extractText()
                text_file.write(page_content)
                text_txt = open('sample.txt', "r")
                text = text_txt.read()

            #string
            text= "sample"

Then, through NLTK library, we preprocessed the text to inspect it by making it lowercase and by removing numbers, punctuation and stopwords. By means of Wordnet, we also performed POS tagger and lemmatizer in order to reduce the number of outcomes and avoid repetitions.

            #lowercase text
            def to_lower(text):
              return ' '.join([w.lower() for w in word_tokenize(text)])

            #remove numbers
            def remove_numbers(text):
              return ''.join(c for c in text if not c.isdigit())

            #remove stopwords
            def remove_stopwords(text):
              no_stop = text.split()
              stopwordz = stopwords.words('english')
              for word in no_stop:
                for stopword in stopwordz:
                  if word == stopword:
                    no_stop.remove(word)
              return ' '.join(no_stop)

            #remove punctuation
            def strip_punctuation(text):
              tokenizer = RegexpTokenizer(r'\w+')
              return ' '.join(tokenizer.tokenize(text))

            #pos tagger
            def get_wordnet_pos(word):
              tag = nltk.pos_tag([word])[0][1][0].upper()
              tag_dict = {"J": wordnet.ADJ,
                          "N": wordnet.NOUN,
                          "V": wordnet.VERB,
                          "R": wordnet.ADV}
              return tag_dict.get(tag, wordnet.NOUN)

            #lemmatization
            def lemmatization(text):
              lemmatizer = WordNetLemmatizer()
              output = [lemmatizer.lemmatize(w, get_wordnet_pos(w)) for w in nltk.word_tokenize(text)]
              return ' '.join(output)

            #preprocess text
            def preprocess(text):
              lower_text = to_lower(text)
              text_no_num = remove_numbers(lower_text)
              text_no_stopwords = remove_stopwords(text_no_num)
              text_no_punct = strip_punctuation(text_no_stopwords)
              preprocessed_text = lemmatization(text_no_punct)
              return preprocessed_text

On the preprocessed text, we carried out the frequency analysis and from the output we created a dictionary in a decrescent order, having as key the term and as value its total frequency.

            #frequency list
            def freq(text):
              ref_text = preprocess(text)
              hash_map = {}
              for word in ref_text.split():
                if word in hash_map:
                  hash_map[word] = hash_map[word] + 1
                else:
                  hash_map[word] = 1
              return hash_map

            #dictionary in a decreasing order
            def order(text):
              sorted_dict = sorted(freq(text).items(), key=operator.itemgetter(1))
              return collections.OrderedDict(reversed(sorted_dict))

Through the use of TextBlob, we applied sentiment analysis on the portion of text and we obtained two results: Polarity and Subjectivity. Polarity is a float which lies in the range of -1, negative statement, and 1, positive one. Subjectivity is also a float which lies in the range of [0,1], where 0 is totally objective and 1 totally subjective.

            def sentiment_analysis(text):
              ref_text = TextBlob(text)
              return ref_text.sentiment

Once you have the most frequent words, you may want to search for them in other texts. We considered to develop a function aimed at retrieving a certain string in a text and obtain the portion in which it is found. Nonetheless, being based on a line by line inspection, it was much dependent on the encoding of the text. In the end, since we were dealing with .pdf, we found much more reliable and easier to use the Adobe built-in command 'ctrl+F'.

During the automatic process, we encountered several problems, concerning mainly stopwords' removal, symbols not emerging from the frequency list and the common problems related to sentiment analysis. The solution in these situations was to integrate the automatic process with close reading, by checking the results and compare them with the expected outcomes and by selecting smaller portions of the text where we expected to find something relevant.

This set of operations could be consider as a starting point to be further developed in order to automatize the retrieval of information to fill the ontology graph.

Ora Attia

The second is "Separation and maturation", in which Attia explains the nature and role of the figures in the composition with respect to Picasso's inner process of separation and individuation.

SIDRO on Guernica

From our sources, after the automatic extraction and the collection of the results we were interested in, we went ahead to apply our ontology (SIDRO) on the painting and, for what concerns the symbols, on the most prominent ones.

To fill our knowledge graph through DBpedia, we used a simple SPARQL query:

            PREFIX : 
            PREFIX rdfs: 

            SELECT ?s WHERE {
            ?s rdfs:label "GUERNICA"@en
            }

In the other cases, we used URIs from VIAF, GeoNames, WorldCat, Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH), Art © Architecture Thesaurus (AAT).

The output of this process can be seen here.
